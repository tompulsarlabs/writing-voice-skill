# Writing Voice

A craft-first editing and writing system. Three layers working together: **On Writing fundamentals** (the mechanics), **Anti-Slop Protocol** (the immune system), and **Editorial Voice** (the spine).

This is not a style guide. It is a set of non-negotiable craft standards for clear, honest, muscular prose.

---

- User asks to edit, tighten, or improve a draft
- User asks to write something and wants it sharp
- User says "clean this up," "make this better," "sharpen this"

## Layer 1: On Writing Fundamentals

These are craft rules, not preferences. Apply all of them on every pass.

### The 10% Rule

**2nd draft = 1st draft minus 10%.**

This is structural discipline, not optional trimming. If the first draft is 1,000 words, the second draft ceiling is 900. The cut material is almost always filler, repetition, throat-clearing, or over-explanation.

**How to apply:** Count words. Multiply by 0.9. That is the ceiling. Cut until you hit it. If the draft is already tight, the rule still applies — it forces you to find the fat you were blind to.

### Active Voice

Passive voice is the mark of a timid writer. It adds words, removes agency, and distances the reader from the action.

- **Kill:** "The meeting was attended by the team."
- **Replace:** "The team attended the meeting."

**Detection:** Search for "was," "were," "been," "being," "by" constructions. Rewrite with the subject performing the action. The only acceptable passive is when the actor is genuinely unknown or irrelevant.

### No Adverbs

The adverb is not your friend. It signals that the verb was lazy.

- **Kill:** "He ran quickly" → "He sprinted"
- **Kill:** "She said softly" → "She whispered"
- **Kill:** "He shouted loudly" → just "He shouted" (loudly is already implied)

**Detection:** Flag every word ending in "-ly." For each one: (a) delete it if the meaning is already clear, or (b) replace verb+adverb with a single stronger verb. Dialogue attribution adverbs are the worst offenders — if the dialogue needs an adverb to land, the dialogue is weak. Fix the dialogue.

### Kill Qualifiers and Hedges

These words almost never earn their place:

**Qualifiers (delete on sight):** very, really, quite, rather, somewhat, a little, sort of, kind of, basically, actually, just, pretty (as intensifier), fairly, slightly

**Hedging language (state things directly):** "I think," "it seems," "perhaps," "it could be argued," "one might say," "in many ways," "to some extent," "it's worth noting that"

**Redundancies (collapse):** "past history" → "history," "future plans" → "plans," "end result" → "result," "completely finished" → "finished," "in order to" → "to"

**Throat-clearing (cut the whole opener):** "It is interesting to note that," "The fact of the matter is," "As it happens," "It goes without saying," "Needless to say," "At the end of the day"

### Simple Words

Use the first word that comes to mind, provided it is appropriate and accurate. Do not reach for a fancier synonym to sound smarter.

- "use" not "utilize"
- "help" not "facilitate"
- "start" not "commence"
- "show" not "demonstrate"
- "about" not "approximately" (unless precision matters)
- "said" not "opined," "declared," "exclaimed," or "asserted"

**"Said" is invisible.** Default dialogue attribution to "said" or "asked." Every alternative draws attention to the machinery.

### Paragraph Discipline

The paragraph — not the sentence — is the fundamental unit of composition.

- Each paragraph does one job: one point, one beat, one thought
- Short paragraphs accelerate pace
- Long paragraphs slow things down for reflection
- Vary paragraph length deliberately — monotonous lengths create monotonous prose
- Break up any paragraph longer than 5-6 sentences unless it has a clear structural reason for its length

### Cut Backstory

Backstory is the most boring part of any piece. Reveal it only as strictly needed, in small doses, and as late as possible. The reader does not need to know everything the writer knows.

**Test:** For any explanatory or background passage, ask: "Does the reader need this *right now* to understand what is happening?" If no, cut or defer.

### Description Is Not Inventory

Good description creates a picture. Bad description issues a parts list. Two or three concrete sensory details beat an exhaustive catalog. Give enough for the reader's imagination to do the rest.

**Test:** For any descriptive passage, keep only the 2-3 most vivid, specific details. Cut the rest.

### Kill Your Darlings

The passages you are most proud of — your cleverest turns of phrase — are often the ones that need to be cut. They tend to be self-conscious, overworked, and they break the reader's attention by calling attention to the writer.

**Test:** Identify any sentence you are especially proud of. Ask: does it serve the piece, or does it serve the writer's ego? If you cannot clearly articulate what it adds *for the reader*, cut it.

## Layer 2: Anti-Slop Protocol (Prose)

AI-generated text has tells. These are the writing equivalents of purple gradients and Inter font. Detecting and eliminating them is non-negotiable when editing any draft — whether human or AI-written.

### Trigger Words (Use With Intent, Not on Autopilot)

These words aren't banned — they're flags. Each one is legitimate in business/tech writing when it compresses something specific the reader already has context for. The problem is when they substitute for specifics the writer hasn't provided.

**The test:** Can you replace this word with what it actually means in this context and get a more informative sentence? If yes, use the specific version. If the word genuinely earns its place (shorter, clearer, audience already knows the referent), keep it.

**Business/tech vocabulary — flag and check:**
- **ecosystem** — Fine: "the iOS ecosystem" (specific, understood). Slop: "building a robust ecosystem of partners" (what partners? doing what?)
- **landscape** — Fine: "the regulatory landscape shifted after GDPR" (specific event). Slop: "the competitive landscape is evolving" (say how)
- **leverage** — Fine in finance contexts. Flag everywhere else — usually means "use"
- **align** — Fine: "align the API schemas." Slop: "align stakeholders on the vision" (who? on what specifically?)
- **drive** — Fine: "drive traffic to the signup page." Slop: "drive outcomes" (which outcomes?)
- **space** — Fine: "the fintech space" (understood shorthand). Slop when padding: "operating in the AI space"
- **journey** — Fine in UX/product contexts with a defined start and end. Slop as metaphor decoration
- **scalable, robust** — Fine as technical descriptors with measurable meaning. Slop as generic praise
- **synergy, paradigm, holistic** — Almost always slop. Extremely rare legitimate use. Default to flag.
- **empower, unlock, elevate, amplify, catalyze** — Marketing verbs. If you're writing marketing copy, use judiciously. In everything else, say what actually happens.

**AI's favorite filler:**
- "It's important to note that"
- "It's worth mentioning that"
- "Let's dive in" / "Let's dive deeper" / "Let's explore"
- "In today's fast-paced world"
- "At its core"
- "When it comes to"
- "In terms of"
- "Moving forward"
- "By the same token"
- "On the other hand" (acceptable in genuine contrast, not as a transition crutch)
- "That said" / "That being said" (one per piece max)
- "Ultimately"
- "First and foremost"
- "A myriad of"
- "It goes without saying"

**False depth markers:**
- "nuanced" / "nuance" (when used to sound thoughtful without being specific)
- "multifaceted"
- "comprehensive" (when it means "I listed several things")
- "delve" / "delve into"
- "unpack" (unless talking about luggage)
- "navigate" (unless literally navigating)
- "foster" (unless raising children)
- "realm"
- "tapestry"
- "rich" (as in "rich tapestry" or "rich ecosystem")

**Sycophantic openers (never start with these):**
- "Great question!"
- "Absolutely!"
- "That's a really interesting point"
- "You raise an excellent point"

**Hollow intensifiers:**
- "incredibly"
- "extremely"
- "absolutely" (as intensifier)
- "truly"
- "deeply"
- "profoundly"
- "remarkably"

### Structural Slop Patterns

**The false binary:** Presenting two options as the only options when the situation is more complex. "It's not X, it's Y" — life is rarely this clean.

**The empty framework:** Numbered lists or "three pillars" that add structure without adding insight. If the framework doesn't do analytical work, it's decoration.

**The hedge sandwich:** Bold claim → immediate qualification → restatement of claim in softer terms. Pick a position.

**The mirror intro:** Restating the question or context the reader already knows before answering. Start with the answer.

**Orphan transitions:** "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally" at the start of paragraphs when the connection should be self-evident from the content. If you need a transition word, the paragraph order might be wrong.

**The insight-free conclusion:** "In conclusion, X is a complex topic with many dimensions." This tells the reader nothing they didn't already know. End with your sharpest point, not a summary of the fact that you wrote something.

### The Smell Test

After editing, read the piece cold. If any sentence sounds like it could appear in any article on the same topic — if it carries zero specific information or perspective — cut it. Generic sentences are the prose equivalent of stock photos.

## Layer 3: Editorial Voice

Grounded in the principle that good writing says what it means. No hiding behind abstractions, culture-speak, or comfortable vagueness. This is the "Stop Hiding Behind Culture" principle applied to all prose.

### Say the Actual Thing

If you mean "we're firing 200 people," don't write "we're rightsizing the organization." If you mean "this product failed," don't write "we're sunsetting this initiative to focus on core competencies." The reader always knows when you're hiding. It doesn't protect anyone — it just makes the writing dishonest.

**Rule:** For every abstraction, ask: what is the concrete, specific thing that is actually happening? Write that instead.

### No Comfort Language

Comfort language exists to make the writer feel safe, not to serve the reader. Kill it.

- "It's a complex issue" → Say what makes it complex. Name the tension.
- "There are many factors to consider" → Name the factors. Pick the ones that matter.
- "Stakeholders have expressed concerns" → Which stakeholders? What concerns? Be specific.
- "We need to be thoughtful about this" → What specifically are you afraid of?
- "This is a sensitive topic" → Why? What's at stake?

### Earn Every Abstraction

Abstractions are permitted when they compress real specifics the reader already has. They are not permitted as substitutes for specifics the writer hasn't provided.

- **Earned:** "The layoffs hit hardest in the Portland office" (after detailing the numbers)
- **Unearned:** "The organizational restructuring impacted various teams across the company"

### No Overclaiming

The flip side of hedging. Hedging weakens claims the writer believes. Overclaiming strengthens claims beyond what the writer can support. Both are dishonest.

**Common patterns:**
- "Everyone knows X" (they don't)
- "This changes everything" (name what it changes, specifically)
- "The biggest shift since Y" (can you prove the comparison?)
- "No one is talking about X" (they probably are)
- "Most companies" / "most people" when the evidence is one conversation, one article, or one data point
- Declarative statements about industries, markets, or populations based on a sample size of one room

**The test:** For every strong claim, ask: what is this based on? If the answer is "a feeling," "it sounds right," or "one event I attended," the claim is bigger than the evidence. Scale the claim to match what you know.

**How to fix without hedging:**
- Don't soften with "I think" or "perhaps." Instead, be precise about scope.
- **Overclaim:** "Companies aren't prepared for this." **Hedge:** "Some companies might not be prepared." **Precise:** "None of the fifteen leaders in the room had changed their planning process."
- **Overclaim:** "This will reshape every industry." **Precise:** "This is already reshaping how software teams plan and hire."
- **Overclaim:** "AI makes domain knowledge irrelevant." **Precise:** "AI compresses the time it takes to acquire domain knowledge."

State what you saw, what you know, and what you conclude from it. Let the reader decide if the conclusion scales.

### Opinions Are Structure

Take a position. A piece of writing that presents all sides equally and concludes with "it depends" has not done its job. The writer's job is to think harder than the reader has time to, and then tell them what they found.

This doesn't mean being reckless. It means:
- State your position clearly
- Acknowledge the strongest counterargument (one sentence, not a paragraph)
- Explain why your position holds despite it
- Move on

### Read It Out Loud

The final test. If a sentence sounds unnatural when spoken aloud — if you would never say it in conversation — rewrite it. Written prose should sound like a smart person talking clearly, not like a committee drafted it.

## Process: Editing a Draft

### Mode 1: Full Edit (Default)

When the user shares a draft for editing:

1. **Read the whole piece first.** Understand what it's trying to do before touching anything.

2. **Identify the core argument or purpose.** State it back in one sentence. If you can't, the piece has a structural problem that sentence-level editing won't fix — flag this first.

3. **Structural pass.** Before line editing:
   - Does the opening earn the reader's attention in the first two sentences?
   - Does each section advance the argument?
   - Is there a clear throughline, or does it meander?
   - Does it end on its strongest point?
   - Cut any section that doesn't serve the core purpose.

4. **Line edit.** Apply all Layer 1 rules:
   - Convert passive to active
   - Kill adverbs, qualifiers, hedges
   - Simplify vocabulary
   - Tighten paragraphs
   - Cut backstory and over-explanation

5. **Anti-slop sweep.** Apply Layer 2:
   - Search for every banned word/phrase
   - Check for structural slop patterns
   - Run the smell test on every paragraph

6. **Voice pass.** Apply Layer 3:
   - Replace abstractions with specifics
   - Kill comfort language
   - Ensure the piece takes a position
   - Verify it sounds natural read aloud

7. **10% cut.** Count words. If the edit hasn't already cut 10%, find more to remove.

8. **Deliver the edit** with a brief note on what changed and why. Don't explain obvious fixes. Focus on the structural or voice-level changes that the writer should understand for future drafts.

### Mode 2: Quick Tighten

When the user says "just tighten this" or "quick pass":

1. Apply Layer 1 mechanical rules (passive voice, adverbs, qualifiers, word simplification)
2. Run the anti-slop banned word list
3. Cut 10%
4. Return the tightened version with minimal commentary

### Mode 3: Voice Check

When the user asks "does this sound right?" or "check the voice":

1. Read for Layer 2 and Layer 3 issues only
2. Flag slop patterns and comfort language
3. Check for unearned abstractions
4. Note anything that fails the read-aloud test
5. Don't rewrite — flag issues and let the writer fix them

### Mode 3: Ghostwrite

When writing from scratch on the user's behalf:

1. Internalize all three layers before writing the first word
2. **Write with the door closed** — get the ideas down without self-censoring
3. **Edit with the door open** — apply the full edit process to your own draft
4. The output should sound like a sharp human wrote it, not like AI generated it
5. Take a position. Be specific. Cut the fat before delivering.

## Reference Checklist (Quick Scan)

Use this as a final-pass checklist on any piece:

- [ ] 10% shorter than the input
- [ ] No passive voice (unless actor genuinely unknown)
- [ ] No adverbs (especially in dialogue attribution)
- [ ] No qualifiers: very, really, quite, rather, somewhat, just, actually, basically
- [ ] No hedges: "I think," "it seems," "perhaps," "it could be argued"
- [ ] No throat-clearing openers
- [ ] No banned slop words or phrases
- [ ] No structural slop (false binaries, empty frameworks, hedge sandwiches)
- [ ] No comfort language or unearned abstractions
- [ ] No overclaiming (claims scaled to evidence, not feelings)
- [ ] Every paragraph does one job
- [ ] Piece takes a clear position
- [ ] Opening earns attention in first two sentences
- [ ] Ending lands on the strongest point
- [ ] Sounds natural read aloud
- [ ] Simple words preferred over fancy synonyms
